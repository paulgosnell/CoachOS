# Founder Burnout: I Was Two Weeks From Quitting Everything

**Last Updated:** November 12, 2025  
**Reading Time:** 9 minutes  
**Author:** Paul - Founder, P0STMAN & Chilled Sites

---

November 2023. I'm sitting in my home office at 2am, staring at my laptop, unable to write a single line of code. Not because I don't know what to build. Because I don't care anymore.

This should be my moment. I'm building at 10x velocity with AI tools. My products are growing. But I feel nothing.

No excitement. No drive. Just this hollow exhaustion that's been building for months.

I'm burned out. And I didn't see it coming.

## The Slow Burn

Burnout doesn't announce itself. There's no alarm that goes off saying "Warning: You're burning out."

It's a slow accumulation of:
- Decisions made while exhausted
- Priorities ignored for urgencies
- Sleep sacrificed for deadlines
- Relationships neglected for growth
- Health deferred for just one more sprint

Each one feels justified in the moment. You're building something. It requires sacrifice. That's just how startups work.

Except it's not sustainable. And your body will eventually call time.

For me, the signs had been there for months:

**Physical:**
- Waking up at 3am with my mind racing
- Constant tension in my shoulders and neck
- Forgetting to eat until evening
- Relying on coffee to function, wine to sleep
- Getting sick more often

**Emotional:**
- Irritable with my wife over nothing
- Avoiding friends who asked how things were going
- Zero patience with contractors or clients
- Feeling nothing when shipping new features
- Dreading Monday mornings (I used to love them)

**Cognitive:**
- Struggling to focus for more than 20 minutes
- Making the same decision multiple times
- Forgetting commitments and conversations
- Unable to think strategically, only tactically
- Paralyzed by simple choices

But I pushed through. Because that's what founders do, right?

## The Breaking Point

The moment I knew I couldn't push anymore: I was working on a client project that should have excited me. Good pay, interesting technical challenge, creative freedom.

I opened the codebase and felt... nothing. No curiosity. No problem-solving drive. Just the overwhelming urge to close my laptop and walk away.

For someone who's coded for 20+ years, who gets energized by building, this was terrifying.

I realized I'd been coasting on momentum for months. The energy was gone. The passion was gone. I was an empty shell going through the motions.

That's when I understood: This wasn't just being tired. This was burnout.

## What Nobody Tells You About Founder Burnout

The standard advice about burnout is useless for founders:

**"Take a vacation"** - With what money? Who runs the business? What about that deadline?

**"Delegate more"** - To whom? I'm a solo founder. The team is freelancers. I'm it.

**"Set boundaries"** - Boundaries with whom? I'm the business. When clients need me, customers need me, the product needs me... there are no boundaries.

**"Practice self-care"** - I know. I should exercise, meditate, eat well. But when you're underwater, adding another thing to your list (even a healthy thing) feels impossible.

Here's what they don't tell you:

**1. Burnout Makes You Stupid**

I was making decisions a version of me from six months ago would have considered obviously wrong. But I couldn't see it. Burnout creates cognitive distortions.

Example: I almost quit a profitable client relationship over a minor communication issue. The "old me" would have solved it in 10 minutes. The burned-out me spiraled into "maybe I should just shut down the whole agency."

**2. Recovery Takes Longer Than You Think**

You don't bounce back in a weekend. I'm talking months, not days.

That weekend at the beach? It'll help for about 48 hours. Then you're right back in it.

Real recovery requires systematic changes to how you work, not temporary escapes from it.

**3. Success Doesn't Prevent Burnout**

I was growing. Revenue was up. Products were shipping. By external measures, I was succeeding.

Burnout doesn't care about your growth metrics. It cares about your sustainable pace.

You can burn out while winning. I did.

**4. Isolation Makes It Worse**

The less I talked to people, the worse it got. I was "too busy" for calls with friends. "Too stressed" for casual conversations.

But isolation is gasoline on the burnout fire. Without outside perspectives, your internal narrative gets progressively more distorted.

**5. It Affects Everyone Around You**

My wife dealt with an irritable, emotionally unavailable partner. My clients got a version of me that was technically competent but creatively flat. My friends got excuses instead of friendship.

Burnout isn't just your problem. It radiates outward.

## The Recovery Framework (What Actually Worked)

Here's what I did to climb out. Not quick fixes. Real changes.

### 1. Acknowledging Reality

First step: Admitting I was burned out.

This sounds simple. It's not. Because admitting burnout feels like admitting failure. Admitting weakness. Admitting you can't handle what you've built.

I wrote in my journal: "I'm burned out. I need help. I can't do this alone anymore."

That sentence changed everything.

### 2. The 20% Reduction

I couldn't take time off. But I could reduce by 20%.

I looked at everything on my plate and asked: "What would happen if I just didn't do this?"

Cut:
- One weekly client meeting (unnecessary)
- Two products I was maintaining (minimal revenue)
- Three hours of admin work (delegated to VA)
- Four Slack communities (mental noise)

That freed up about 8 hours a week. Not transformative, but enough to breathe.

### 3. The Hard Conversations

I had overdue conversations with three clients about scope and expectations. I was scared they'd leave.

They didn't. They appreciated the honesty. Two actually reduced their demands without me asking.

Turns out, being honest about capacity creates better relationships than pretending you're fine.

### 4. The 2pm Rule

No creative or strategic work after 2pm.

After 2pm, I was running on fumes. Any attempt at creative work was low-quality and took twice as long.

So I stopped trying. After 2pm: Admin, email, simple tasks. That's it.

This single rule improved my output quality by 50%. I was working less but producing more.

### 5. The Mandatory Walks

Every day, 30 minutes, non-negotiable. No podcasts, no calls, no "making it productive."

Just walking.

This felt indulgent and wasteful. It was actually essential. My best thinking happened on these walks.

### 6. The Weekly Check-In

Every Sunday evening, 15 minutes:
- What went well this week?
- What drained me?
- What can I cut or delegate next week?
- What do I actually need?

This prevented the slow slide back into overload. The moment I noticed something unsustainable, I addressed it.

### 7. The Emergency Coaching

I needed someone to reality-check my thinking. Someone who knew my context but wasn't in my head.

I tried scheduling traditional coaching. The first available session was 10 days away. By then, I'd already made three major decisions in isolation.

What I actually needed: Someone I could text Tuesday at 3pm when I was spiraling. Someone who remembered last week's conversation without me having to repeat context.

This is why I'm building Coach OS. Because burnout doesn't respect your calendar.

## The Warning Signs (What I Wish I'd Caught Earlier)

If you're experiencing three or more of these, you're heading for burnout:

**Energy Markers:**
- Waking up tired regardless of sleep
- Coffee doesn't work anymore
- Sunday evening dread
- Counting down to Friday by Tuesday
- Physical exhaustion that doesn't improve with rest

**Emotional Markers:**
- Cynicism about your work
- Irritability with people you love
- Nothing feels fun anymore
- Avoiding social situations
- Emotional flatness (not sad, just... nothing)

**Cognitive Markers:**
- Struggling to start tasks
- Decision paralysis
- Forgetting obvious things
- Can't think long-term, only short-term
- Making uncharacteristic mistakes

**Behavioral Markers:**
- Working more, achieving less
- Skipping meals or eating poorly
- Relying on substances (alcohol, caffeine)
- Neglecting physical health
- Withdrawing from relationships

The tricky part: These develop slowly. You adapt to each level of dysfunction, then it becomes your new normal.

## What I'd Do Differently

Looking back, here's what could have prevented the crash:

**1. Talk About It Earlier**

I kept the struggle internal for months. If I'd talked to peers, a coach, or even my wife about what was happening, I could have course-corrected earlier.

Suffering in silence doesn't make you stronger. It makes you stupid.

**2. Set Hard Boundaries**

"I'll work less when things calm down" is a trap. Things never calm down.

I should have established:
- No work after 7pm (instead of "until I finish")
- One full day off per week (instead of "when I can")
- Maximum 45 hours per week (instead of "whatever it takes")

**3. Cut Ruthlessly**

I was doing 15 things okay instead of 5 things excellently.

The products that were dragging me down? They made Â£200/month. The mental overhead? Massive.

I should have killed them six months earlier.

**4. Invest in Support Earlier**

I waited until crisis before seeking coaching. By then, I was making decisions from a compromised state.

If I'd had regular strategic support, I could have prevented the spiral instead of recovering from it.

**5. Trust My Gut**

I knew I was overextended. I knew the pace wasn't sustainable. I knew I needed changes.

But I ignored the signals because "startups are hard" and "everyone struggles."

There's a difference between hard and unsustainable. I should have trusted myself to know which I was experiencing.

## The Ongoing Reality

I'd love to tell you I'm fully recovered and never get close to burnout anymore.

That would be a lie.

What's true: I'm better at catching the early signs. I'm faster at course-correcting. I'm more willing to have hard conversations early.

Some weeks are still brutal. But I have systems in place to prevent the slow accumulation that led to the 2am laptop stare.

**Current practices:**
- Weekly reflection (non-negotiable)
- Hard stop at 45 hours/week
- Mandatory one day completely off
- Regular walks without productivity
- Proactive reduction when I notice strain
- Quick access to strategic thinking support

The last one is key. When I'm feeling overwhelmed, I don't wait for Thursday's coaching session. I check in that day, process it, and move forward.

## For Founders Currently Burning Out

If you're reading this and recognizing yourself:

**You're not weak.** Burnout isn't a personal failing. It's a system failure.

**You're not alone.** 72% of founders experience burnout. You're in the majority, not the minority.

**It won't fix itself.** Hoping it'll get better without changing anything is denial.

**Small changes matter.** You don't need to quit or take six months off. You need systematic small reductions and support.

**Ask for help.** From peers, coaches, partners, or professionals. Doing this alone is what got you here.

## The Ironic Truth

I'm building Coach OS because I needed it and it didn't exist.

What I needed wasn't expensive monthly retainers with coaches I had to schedule three weeks in advance.

What I needed was someone who knew my context, understood my situation, and was available when the wheels were coming off.

Not next Tuesday. Not when things calmed down. Right then.

If you're where I was - successful on paper but empty inside - you need that too.

The good news: You don't have to wait until you're at 2am laptop-staring to address it.

The early signs are enough. Trust them.
